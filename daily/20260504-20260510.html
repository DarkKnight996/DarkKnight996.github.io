[arXiv260504] Replication in Graph Partitioning and Scheduling Problems

arXiv
sysparallel computinggraph partitioning, DAG scheduling, replication, hypergraph, integer linear programming, heuristic
Authors
Pál András Papp, Toni Böhnlein, A. N. Yzelman
Institution
Huawei Zurich Research Center

This paper systematically analyzes the impact of allowing node replication in graph partitioning and DAG scheduling problems for parallel computing. The authors show theoretically that replication increases approximation difficulty for partitioning but not for scheduling, and experimentally demonstrate significant cost reductions (17%-65% for hypergraphs and 11.61%-58.17% for DAGs) using ILP formulations and a novel heuristic.

[arXiv260504] Cloud Is Closer Than It Appears: Revisiting the Tradeoffs of Distributed Real-Time Inference

arXiv
mlsyscloud inferencedistributed inference, real-time latency, analytical model, cloud vs. on-device, emergency braking, autonomous driving, queueing delay, throughput, safety constraints
Authors
Pragya Sharma, Hang Qiu, Mani Srivastava
Institution
University of California Los Angeles (UCLA), University of California Riverside

This paper formally analyzes the latency of distributed real-time DNN inference for CPS, modeling it as a function of sensing frequency, platform throughput, network delay, and safety constraints. Using emergency braking in autonomous driving as a case study, it shows that cloud-based inference, when provisioned with high-throughput compute, can meet safety margins more reliably than on-device inference. The conclusion challenges traditional design assumptions by demonstrating that the cloud can be a preferred inference location for latency-sensitive control tasks.

Authors
Dongxin Guo, Jikun Wu, Siu Ming Yiu
Institution
The University of Hong Kong, Brain Investing Limited, Stellaris AI Limited

SAGA proposes a shift from request-level to program-level scheduling for AI agent inference on GPU clusters, treating entire agent workflows as schedulable units. It introduces Agent Execution Graphs to predict KV cache reuse, session-affinity batching with work stealing, and an Agent Fair Share fairness metric. Evaluated on a 64-GPU cluster, SAGA reduces task completion time by 1.64x over vLLM while improving GPU memory utilization, demonstrating workflow-aware scheduling is essential for efficient compound AI serving.

The paper adapts a machine learning model for computational fluid dynamics to run on Graphcore's IPU platform, using custom TensorFlow and the Poplar SDK. [arXiv260504] Token Arena: A Continuous Benchmark Unifying Energy and Cognition in AI Inference

arXiv

Authors
Yuxuan Gao, Megan Wang, Yi Ling Yu
Institution
University of Pennsylvania, Columbia University, OpenMesh AI

TokenArena introduces a continuous benchmark for AI inference that evaluates endpoints (provider-model-SKU triples) on five axes—speed, latency, workload-blended price, effective context, and live quality—and synthesizes them into composite metrics including energy and cost per correct answer. Testing across 78 endpoints and 12 model families reveals that the same model can vary by up to 12.5 accuracy points and 6.2x in energy efficiency across different endpoints, and that workload-aware pricing significantly reorders leaderboard rankings. The framework is released as an open methodology for reproducible endpoint comparison.

Authors

Institution

This paper introduces Tempus, a temporal GEMM framework for AMD Versal AI Edge SoCs that achieves scalability through iterative graph execution and data tiling on a fixed 16-core compute block, rather than spatial scaling. By using high-speed cascade streaming and a deadlock-free DATAFLOW protocol, it achieves 607 GOPS at 10.677 W and demonstrates a 211.2x higher prominence factor than the leading spatial SOTA (ARIES). The framework establishes a sustainable, scalable foundation for edge LLM inference with zero URAM/DSP utilization and significant reductions in core, power, and I/O demands.

Institution
North Carolina State University, University of Maryland, University of Louisville, University of Michigan, University of Miami

This paper introduces a network digital untwinning framework for selectively removing deprecated digital twin contributions while preserving model integrity. The framework includes two mechanisms: Single Request Untwinning (SRU) using geographically-aware connectivity metrics and rollback with Gaussian noise, and Parallel Request Untwinning (PRU) clustering similar twins for coordinated removal. Authors
Byungsoo Oh, Rachee Singh
Institution
Cornell University

The paper identifies hidden serialization in proxy-based RDMA transports as a key bottleneck for multi-node MoE inference, where ordering requirements between tile transfers and completion signals cause NIC pipeline stalls. To address this, the authors propose Perseus, which uses decoupled signaling and NIC-side ordering to eliminate serialization, achieving up to 10.3× speedup on proxy-based transports and matching or exceeding GPU-direct performance, showing that serialization—not the transport choice—limits multi-node megakernel performance.

[arXiv260504] or Not : A Tale of Three Algorithms for Streaming: Covariance Estimation after Welford and Chan-Golub-LeVeque

arXiv
aionline statisticsstreaming covariance, Welford algorithm, Chan–Golub–LeVeque, Gram algorithm, conformal prediction, numerical stability
Authors
Felix Reichel
Institution
Lund School of Economics and Management (LUSEM)

This paper unifies three streaming covariance estimation algorithms—Gram, Welford, and Chan–Golub–LeVeque—on algebraic, numerical, and statistical grounds, showing they yield identical results in exact arithmetic but differ in numerical stability and suitability for distributed settings. It introduces a novel conformal prediction framework to provide finite-sample, distribution-free confidence intervals for covariance entries during streaming. Experiments confirm Gram is fastest for batch computing, Welford is most robust to cancellation, CGL excels in distributed environments, and the conformal intervals maintain nominal coverage.
